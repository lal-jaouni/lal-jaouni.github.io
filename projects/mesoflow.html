MesoFlow LIMS

A full-stack laboratory information management system built for electrochemiluminescence bioassay experiments. Covers the entire workflow from plate layout design through automated image processing and quantitative reporting.

Role Sole Developer & Architect
Organization Meso Scale Diagnostics
Scale ~55,000 lines of code

The Problem

Running ECL bioassay experiments involves a lot of moving parts: designing plate layouts, tracking reagents, configuring instruments, processing raw images, and generating quantitative results. Before MesoFlow, these steps were handled by a patchwork of spreadsheets, standalone scripts, and manual bookkeeping — each experiment required hours of data wrangling before you could even look at results.

The image processing piece was especially painful. Aligning spot grids on raw plate images, correcting for reader-specific artifacts, and extracting per-spot intensities required computational methods that were too slow for interactive use and too fragile for batch processing.

What I Built

Experiment Lifecycle Management

A complete workflow system where scientists can design 96-well plate layouts with a drag-and-click editor, configure multi-tab reagent settings, build step-by-step experimental workflows, and track experiments from setup through completion with automatic status tracking and validation.

Computational Image Processing Pipeline

The core technical challenge. Each plate image needs a precise grid alignment to extract per-spot signal intensities. I built a two-phase grid search: a coarse pass (~13,000 iterations covering translation and rotation) followed by a fine pass (~7,700 iterations at sub-pixel precision). Together, that's ~21,000 evaluations per image.

To make that practical, I optimized the alignment engine with Numba JIT compilation, achieving 20-100x speedup over pure Python. The pipeline also handles MAD-based cosmic ray detection, truncated circle masks for Kivu plate geometry, scale-aware processing across 1x-16x binning levels, and reader-specific gain corrections for three different instrument types (Q60, SQ120, S600).

Multi-Reader Support

Each MSD plate reader has different sensor geometries: the Q60 reads a single 1040×1040 sector, the SQ120 reads four 512×512 sectors, and the S600 reads sixteen sectors. MesoFlow handles all three with reader-specific calibration, gain corrections, and spot factor adjustments, producing consistent results across instruments.

Quantitative Analysis

Built a full scientific validation suite: binning experiments across 5 levels on 2 readers (570+ per-image measurements), SNR and detection limit calculations, coefficient of variation analysis, and gain cross-calibration. Results feed into 8+ publication-quality plot types for reporting.

Hardware Integration

Integrated directly with the Mini Collet Dispenser via a custom serial driver (842 lines), with step-by-step workflow execution, 5 concurrent instrument tabs, and operator tracking.

What This Taught Me

MesoFlow taught me that scientific software lives or dies by how well it models the actual workflow of the people using it. The hardest problems weren't algorithmic — they were understanding why a bench scientist configures a plate layout the way they do, why certain validation steps can't be skipped, and how to surface results in a way that maps to how they think about their experiments. The Numba optimization work was technically satisfying, but the real value was in making the whole system feel like a natural extension of the lab workflow.
