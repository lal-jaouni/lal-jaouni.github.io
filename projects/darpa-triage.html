DARPA Triage Challenge

Predicting which life-saving interventions a trauma patient will need from streaming multi-modal physiological data and clinical records, before the interventions become emergencies.

Role ML Research Engineer
Team MSAI Labs (DARPA-funded)
Duration January 2026 – Present
Data Scale 327 GB · 7,000+ patients · multiple trauma centers
Scope 56 experiments across 8 research objectives

The Problem

In trauma care, the first hours determine survival. Clinicians must rapidly decide which life-saving interventions a patient needs -- from airway management and damage control procedures to medications and vascular access. The DARPA Triage Challenge asks whether machine learning can predict these interventions from streaming multi-modal physiological data before they become emergencies.

The data is messy, multi-modal, and massive. Electronic health records from dozens of clinical tables, continuous physiological waveforms and vital signs, and lab results, all collected across multiple trauma centers with different recording conventions and schemas.

What I Built

Signal Processing Pipeline

Built an end-to-end pipeline processing multiple physiological signal types at up to 240 Hz. Each signal goes through calibration, temporal alignment, quality assessment, and feature extraction.

A critical early discovery: ICU sensors connect at different times, creating significant temporal misalignment between signals in the same recording session. Fixing this with chunk-by-chunk timestamp intersection was the single largest performance gain of the project -- larger than any architecture change.

Rather than discarding noisy segments (which loses the sickest patients), I built a 7-metric continuous quality scoring system and fed quality scores as model inputs. This recovered patient coverage from 60% to 94% while maintaining prediction accuracy.

Feature Engineering (2,200+ Features)

Designed 12 distinct feature families capturing different aspects of hemodynamic physiology:

  • Clinical hemodynamics: Blood pressure dynamics, shock indices, heart rate variability, and waveform morphology features
  • Wavelet decomposition: Multi-level discrete wavelet transform capturing respiratory modulation, pulse dynamics, and vascular tone
  • Temporal trends: Rolling window statistics tracking hemodynamic trajectory over multiple time horizons
  • Complexity analysis: Sample entropy and nonlinear dynamics features validated for detecting hemodynamic instability
  • Extended statistics: Exponential moving averages, acceleration, trend reliability, and robust percentile features
  • EHR features: Extracted from dozens of clinical tables across multiple hospital sites with schema harmonization

A multi-phase feature selection pipeline (mutual information filtering, correlation clustering, SHAP stability analysis, permutation importance validation) reduced 2,200 features to 22–40 validated clusters per prediction target.

Transfer Learning for Waveform Prediction

Adapted a nested U-Net architecture from image segmentation to 1D physiological waveform prediction. Pre-trained on millions of external physiological segments using a novel lag-invariant loss function that handles temporal misalignment between clinical sensors.

The loss combines four components: Fourier correlation (completely phase-invariant), spectral magnitude matching, beat peak amplitude ranking (position-independent), and lag-tolerant Pearson correlation. This enabled effective cross-domain transfer despite uncorrectable hardware timing differences.

Per-Category Prediction Optimization

Different intervention categories have fundamentally different pathophysiology and predictive signals. A single global model compromises on all categories.

I trained independent gradient boosting models per category, each with its own feature selection, hyperparameter optimization (50 Bayesian trials via Optuna), and evaluation. This per-category approach significantly outperformed a single global model.

Streaming Prediction System

Built a stateful streaming predictor that processes physiological segments in real time, maintaining patient state across a rolling window. Achieves real-time prediction well within the challenge's latency budget.

Technical Differentiators

Data Quality > Architecture

Temporal alignment fixes yielded a larger improvement than any model architecture change. In clinical ML, the data pipeline is the model.

Quality as Features

Signal quality scores fed as continuous model inputs rather than binary discard gates, dramatically improving patient coverage over traditional quality gating.

Domain-Aware Loss Design

Custom loss functions encoding clinical priorities: severity-weighted errors, phase-invariant waveform comparison, and beat-level amplitude matching.

Explainability-First Ranking

Feature importance validated through two independent methods (TreeSHAP stability + held-out permutation importance), not just single-method rankings.

Experiment Rigor

Conducted dozens of systematic experiments across multiple research objectives, each with formal configuration, reproducible seeds, and tracked results. Built a config-driven experiment framework enabling rapid hypothesis testing with schema validation and automated reporting.

Produced publication-quality scientific reports using Quarto with LaTeX typesetting, multi-panel figures, and statistical validation. Every modeling decision was backed by ablation analysis and SHAP-based clinical interpretability.

Why This Matters

Triage decisions currently depend on a clinician's experience and a handful of vital signs. If these models work, they could give first responders and ICU teams an early warning system backed by thousands of similar cases and continuous physiological monitoring. This is especially critical in mass casualty events where experienced clinicians are spread thin.

More broadly, this project demonstrates what production healthcare AI requires: handling messy multi-site clinical data, building quality assurance for physiological signals, designing evaluation metrics that account for rare clinical events, and producing explainable predictions that clinicians can trust in high-stakes settings.

The biggest lesson: in clinical ML, data engineering matters more than model architecture. The single largest improvement came from fixing temporal alignment between sensors, not from a fancier neural network. Understanding the data, its quirks, and its clinical meaning is what separates useful healthcare AI from academic benchmarks.
